Lesson: Tu vs. Vous

15 minutes
Textbook Reference: Pages 4-5 - Vie et culture: Tu et vous

Teacher Lecture: Understanding Tu and Vous

Alright everyone, let's talk about one of the most important distinctions you'll make when speaking French - the difference between tu and vous. Both of these words mean "you" in English, but in French, they carry very different meanings and using the wrong one can actually be quite awkward or even insulting!

Think of tu as the informal, friendly "you." You use it with people you're close to - your friends, your family members, children, and even your pets! It signals intimacy, familiarity, and equality. When you use tu with someone, you're essentially saying, "We're on the same level, we're comfortable with each other."

Now vous serves two purposes. First, it's the formal "you" - the respectful way to address someone you don't know well, someone older than you, someone in a position of authority like your professor or your boss, or anyone in a professional setting. It creates a certain distance, shows respect, and maintains social boundaries. Second, vous is always used when addressing more than one person - even if they're all your best friends! So if you're talking to two or more people, it's always vous, no matter how close you are.

Here's what's interesting about French culture - the French take this distinction very seriously. If you use tu with someone you should address as vous, you might come across as disrespectful or presumptuous. But if you use vous with someone who expects tu, you might seem cold or distant. There's even a verb for switching from vous to tu - tutoyer - and sometimes people will explicitly suggest it: "On peut se tutoyer?" meaning "Can we use tu with each other?"

My advice for you as beginners? When in doubt, start with vous. It's better to be a little too formal than too familiar. Pay attention to how French people address you, and follow their lead. If someone switches to tu with you, you can safely do the same. And remember - age matters! If someone is significantly older than you, stick with vous until they tell you otherwise. Let's look at some specific examples now.

The Important Distinction:

French has two ways to say "you" - and choosing the right one is crucial!

üåç Cultural Note: Handshakes & La Bise

Handshake: Used in formal situations, business settings, or when meeting someone for the first time.

La bise: Cheek kisses (usually 2, but varies by region!) used among friends, family, and social acquaintances. You lean in and make a kissing sound near each cheek.

When in doubt? Wait to see what the other person does, or stick with a handshake!

Now that we've covered greetings, let's talk about how to actually respond when someone asks you Comment √ßa va? or Comment allez-vous? Pronunciation: French Alphabet

15 minutes
Textbook Reference: Pages 8-9 - Sons et lettres: L'alphabet

Teacher Lecture: Mastering French Pronunciation

Alright class, let's dive into the French alphabet! Now, you might be thinking, "It's just 26 letters, same as English, how hard can it be?" Well, here's the thing - while the letters look the same, they sound quite different! And mastering these sounds is absolutely crucial because French spelling and pronunciation have a very specific relationship.

First, let's talk about why this matters. In French, when you're on the phone, filling out forms, spelling your name at a hotel, or giving your email address, you need to be able to spell things out loud clearly. And unlike English where you might say "M as in Mike" or "B as in Boy," French speakers typically just say the letter names very clearly. So you need to know these sounds inside and out.

Let me point out the letters that trip up English speakers the most. First, the letter E - in English we say "ee," but in French it's more like "euh," a very neutral vowel sound made in the middle of your mouth. The letter G is "zhay" - notice that soft "zh" sound at the beginning, like the "s" in "pleasure." The letter H is "ahsh" - and here's a fun fact: H is almost always silent in French words! You don't pronounce it when you're saying French words, but when spelling, you clearly say "ahsh."

Now the famous French R - it sounds like "air" but with that distinctive French R sound in the middle, that guttural sound from the back of your throat. It's not rolled like Spanish; it's more like you're gently clearing your throat. Don't worry if it feels weird at first - it takes practice! The letter W is fascinating - it's called "doo-bluh-vay," which literally means "double V." Why? Because when you look at the letter W, it's literally two V's! The French saw it that way, and the name stuck.

And finally Y - we call it "ee-grek" meaning "Greek i," because it was borrowed from Greek and sounds like the letter I. Speaking of which, I in French sounds like our English "ee," while U makes that very French "ew" sound where you round your lips tightly - it's the sound that makes French sound so distinctly French!

We also need to talk about accent marks - those little symbols above letters that completely change how words are pronounced and what they mean. French has five accent marks: the acute accent on √©, the grave accent on √®, the circumflex on √™, the dieresis or tr√©ma on √´, and the cedilla or c√©dille under √ß. These aren't optional - they're part of correct spelling and they change pronunciation. We'll learn all of these today! Let's start by singing the alphabet together.

Teacher Lecture: Learning to Count in French

Alright class, today we're going to learn one of the most practical skills in any language - counting! You'll need numbers constantly in France: giving your phone number, asking prices, telling someone your age, reading addresses, catching the right bus or metro. So let's dive right in.

The good news is that French numbers from 0 to 20 are straightforward - you just need to memorize them. But I'll give you some tips to help. Notice that some numbers sound similar to English because both languages came from Latin roots. Six sounds like "cease," sept is "set," huit is "wheat," neuf is "nuff," and dix is "deese." These associations can help!

Once you get to 21, French becomes very logical - you literally say the number as it appears. Vingt-et-un is "twenty-and-one" which is 21, vingt-deux is "twenty-two," and so on. That et meaning "and" only appears in 21, 31, 41, 51, 61, and 71. For all other numbers, you just use a hyphen. Let's learn these systematically, and I want you to repeat after me - pronunciation is key!

Lesson: Classroom Objects

15 minutes
Textbook Reference: Pages 14-15 - Dans la salle de classe

Teacher Lecture: Objects Around Us

Now let's learn vocabulary for things you see every day in this classroom! These words are incredibly useful because I'll be using them constantly to give you instructions. When I say Ouvrez votre livre, you need to know that livre means "book." When I say Prenez un stylo, you need to know that stylo means "pen."

One important thing to notice: every French noun has a gender - either masculine or feminine. We show this with the articles un (masculine) or une (feminine). For example, un livre (a book) is masculine, but une porte (a door) is feminine. These genders don't always make logical sense - you just have to memorize them. Always learn the article with the noun!

Teacher Lecture: Following Instructions in French

Starting today, I'm going to begin giving some of my classroom instructions in French! Don't worry - I'll use gestures and demonstrations so you understand. But it's important that you learn these common commands because you'll hear them every single day in this class. The more French you hear, the faster you'll learn. These are all in the vous form because I'm addressing the whole class.

üéØ TPR Practice (Total Physical Response)6 min

Instructions: Everyone stand up! We're going to play a French version of Simon Says.

I'll give you commands in French, and you'll perform the actions. If I say Jacques a dit (Jacques said - our French "Simon says") before the command, you do it. If I don't say Jacques a dit, don't move! If you mess up, sit down. Let's see who the last person standing is!

Example commands:

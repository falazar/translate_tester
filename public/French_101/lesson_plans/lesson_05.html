Cultural Lesson: French Greetings in Context

15 minutes
Cultural Focus: Understanding how French people actually greet each other in real life - beyond just the words!

The Art of French Greetings

We've learned the words for greetings this week - Bonjour, Salut, Bonsoir - but how do French people actually greet each other? It's not just about what you say; it's also about the physical gesture that goes with it. And this can vary quite a bit depending on who you're greeting and where you are in France!

Let me break down the three main greeting styles you'll encounter in France:

1. The Handshake (La Poign√©e de main)

When to use: Business settings, formal situations, meeting someone for the first time (especially if they're older or in a position of authority)

How to do it: One firm handshake (not too aggressive!), maintain eye contact, say Enchant√©(e) when meeting someone new

Cultural notes:

  • In professional settings, you should shake hands every morning when you arrive at work - even with colleagues you saw yesterday!
  • Same thing when leaving - shake hands again at the end of the day
  • This might feel formal to Americans, but it's standard professional courtesy in France

James's experience: When James meets his professors at the Sorbonne, he shakes their hands and says Bonjour, Professeur Moreau. Enchant√©!

2. The Cheek Kiss (La Bise)

When to use: Friends, family, informal settings, social situations

How to do it: Lean in, touch cheeks (or just get close without actually touching), make a kissing sound - but here's the tricky part: how many times?

Regional differences - this is where it gets interesting!

  • Paris: Usually 2 kisses (right cheek first, then left)
  • Provence: Usually 2 kisses
  • Brittany: Can be 1, 2, or even 3!
  • Loire Valley: Sometimes 4 kisses!

Pro tip: When you're unsure, just follow the other person's lead. They'll set the pace, and you just mirror what they do. Don't stress about it - even French people from different regions sometimes have to figure it out together!

Important boundaries:

  • La bise is typically between people who know each other, not strangers
  • In professional/workplace settings, stick with handshakes unless your colleagues initiate la bise
  • If you're uncomfortable, a smile and a verbal greeting is always acceptable
  • Men typically shake hands with each other rather than doing la bise (unless they're family or very close friends)

James's experience: When James meets his roommate Pierre's friends Sophie and Marie at a caf√©, they greet him with la bise: Salut James! √áa va? He was nervous at first, but they guided him through it!

3. The Wave or Verbal Greeting Only

When to use: Across a distance, in a hurry, casual acknowledgment, or when hands are full

How to do it: A simple Salut! or Coucou! with a wave

Cultural notes:

  • Perfectly acceptable to just say Bonjour! when passing someone on the street
  • Coucou is a very informal, friendly greeting - like "Hey!" in English
  • It's polite to acknowledge people you pass in smaller towns (less common in Paris)

Important Cultural Context: Personal Space

You might notice that French people tend to stand closer during conversations than Americans do. This isn't considered invasive - it's just the normal conversational distance in French culture. If someone steps closer while talking to you, they're not being aggressive; they're just being French!

Similarly, making eye contact during greetings is important. It's considered polite and shows you're genuinely acknowledging the person. Looking away or down might come across as rude or disinterested.

Teacher Lecture: The French Counting System

Alright everyone, now we're getting into the interesting part of French numbers! The pattern continues logically through the 30s, 40s, 50s, and 60s - you'll pick these up quickly. But then something fascinating happens at 70. The French counting system takes a unique turn that makes it different from most other languages.

Let me explain: In standard French (what we call "French from France"), instead of having a word for 70, they say soixante-dix which literally means "sixty-ten." So 71 is "sixty-eleven" (soixante-et-onze), 72 is "sixty-twelve" (soixante-douze), and so on up to 79 which is "sixty-nineteen" (soixante-dix-neuf).

Then at 80, instead of a single word, French uses quatre-vingts - literally "four-twenties." This is a leftover from when people counted in groups of 20! So 81 is "four-twenties-one" (quatre-vingt-un), 82 is "four-twenties-two," and so on. And here's the final twist: 90 is quatre-vingt-dix - "four-twenties-ten!" So 99, the highest two-digit number, is quatre-vingt-dix-neuf - "four-twenties-ten-nine."

Now, fun cultural note: In Belgium and Switzerland, they actually simplify this! They say septante for 70, octante or huitante for 80, and nonante for 90. Much more logical! But we'll learn the French system since that's what you'll encounter most often. Let's practice these step by step.

  • 30 - trente
  • 40 - quarante
  • 50 - cinquante

  • 70 - soixante-dix (sixty-ten)
  • 80 - quatre-vingts (four-twenties) - Note: -s only when exactly 80!
  • 90 - quatre-vingt-dix (four-twenty-ten)
  • 100 - cent

Teacher Lecture: The French Week

Let's learn the days of the week - you'll need these constantly for talking about your schedule, making plans, and understanding when things happen. Here's something interesting: in French, the days of the week are NOT capitalized unless they're at the beginning of a sentence. Also, notice that many French day names come from Latin and Roman gods, just like in English!

Days of the Week (Les jours de la semaine):

  • lundi - Monday (from Latin "Luna" - Moon day)
  • mardi - Tuesday (from Mars - Mars day)
  • mercredi - Wednesday (from Mercury - Mercury day)
  • jeudi - Thursday (from Jupiter - Jupiter day)
  • vendredi - Friday (from Venus - Venus day)
  • samedi - Saturday (from Saturn - Saturn day)
  • dimanche - Sunday (from Latin "Dominicus" - Lord's day)

Useful Expressions:

  • Quel jour sommes-nous? - What day is it?
  • Nous sommes lundi. - It's Monday.
  • Aujourd'hui, c'est mardi. - Today is Tuesday.
  • Le lundi - On Mondays (every Monday)
  • lundi prochain - next Monday

If someone asks where you're from, you might add a sentence about what it's like there. If they ask what you study, explain why you chose that subject. Cultural Topic: The Francophone World

10 minutes
Textbook Reference: Pages 20-21 - Le monde francophone

Teacher Lecture: French Around the World

I want to share something exciting with you: when you learn French, you're not just learning to communicate with people in France. French is spoken by over 300 million people across five continents! It's an official language in 29 countries and is the second most studied language in the world after English. Let me show you the incredible diversity of the French-speaking world.

In Europe, besides France, you have Belgium, Switzerland, Luxembourg, and Monaco. In North America, there's Quebec in Canada and parts of Louisiana in the United States, plus several Caribbean islands like Haiti, Martinique, and Guadeloupe. In Africa - this is huge - over 30 African countries speak French, including Senegal, Ivory Coast, Morocco, Algeria, Tunisia, Madagascar, and many more. In fact, Africa has more French speakers than Europe!

Then you have French territories in the Pacific like French Polynesia and New Caledonia, and in South America there's French Guiana. Each place has its own accent, its own expressions, its own culture - but they all share this beautiful language. The French-speaking world is called la Francophonie, and it's incredibly diverse and vibrant. Learning French opens doors to all these cultures!

Major Francophone Regions:

  • Europe: France, Belgium, Switzerland, Luxembourg, Monaco
  • North America: Quebec (Canada), Haiti, Martinique, Guadeloupe
  • Africa: Senegal, Morocco, Algeria, Tunisia, Ivory Coast, Cameroon, Madagascar, and 20+ more countries
  • Asia & Middle East: Lebanon, Vietnam (historical influence)
  • Pacific: French Polynesia, New Caledonia

Why is French So Global?

French isn't just spoken in France! It's a truly global language spoken on five continents. Here's why French became so widespread:

  • French colonial history (17th-20th centuries) - France and Belgium established colonies across Africa, Southeast Asia, the Caribbean, and the Pacific, bringing the French language to these regions. While colonialism ended, French often remained as an official language.
  • French as a diplomatic language - For centuries, French was the language of international diplomacy. Even today, diplomatic documents are often written in both English and French.
  • Official language of major organizations - French is an official language of the United Nations, European Union, NATO, the Olympics, FIFA, and many other international organizations. This means all official documents must be available in French.
  • Growing importance in Africa - Here's something surprising: Africa is now home to more French speakers than Europe! Countries like Democratic Republic of Congo, Ivory Coast, and Cameroon have huge French-speaking populations. By 2050, it's projected there will be over 700 million French speakers, with most living in Africa. This makes French an increasingly important language for business and development.

Fun fact: French is the only language besides English spoken on all five continents! Learning French opens doors to Europe, Africa, North America, Asia, and Oceania.
